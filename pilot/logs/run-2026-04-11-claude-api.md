## Query 06: What is the MAU Trap in startup metrics?

**Tokens:** 2,224 in / 408 out  |  **Stop reason:** `end_turn`

**Response:**

The MAU Trap is a concept in startup metrics that warns against relying too heavily on Monthly Active Users (MAU) as a key performance indicator. Here's what it means:

**The Core Problem:**
The MAU Trap occurs when startups focus on growing their total monthly active user count while ignoring more important underlying metrics. A growing MAU number can mask serious problems with the business, such as:

1. **Poor retention** - New users may be joining, but if existing users are leaving at a high rate, the business isn't sustainable. You might be constantly replacing churned users rather than actually growing.

2. **Low engagement** - MAU only measures if someone used the product at least once in a month, not how much value they're getting. Users who logged in once vs. users who use the product daily both count the same.

3. **Weak unit economics** - A large MAU doesn't necessarily translate to revenue or profitability. You could have millions of users but still be losing money on each one.

**Why it's dangerous:**
- MAU is easy to manipulate or game through tactics like aggressive marketing, viral gimmicks, or promotional offers that bring in low-quality users
- It can create a false sense of success and lead to poor strategic decisions
- Investors and stakeholders may be misled about the actual health of the business

**Better alternatives:**
Startups should complement MAU with metrics like:
- **Retention cohorts** - How many users stick around over time
- **DAU/MAU ratio** - Measures engagement (daily active users / monthly active users)
- **Revenue or revenue per user**
- **Net retention rate**
- **Customer lifetime value (LTV)**

The key lesson is that vanity metrics like raw MAU growth should always be examined alongside metrics that indicate actual user value and business sustainability.

**Response:**

The **Four Lenses VC Framework** is a venture capital evaluation framework used to assess startup investment opportunities. While there are variations, the framework typically examines four key dimensions:

## The Four Lenses:

1. **People/Team**
   - Quality and experience of founders and management team
   - Track record, domain expertise, and ability to execute
   - Team dynamics and complementary skill sets

2. **Market/Opportunity**
   - Market size and growth potential (TAM - Total Addressable Market)
   - Market timing and trends
   - Customer pain points and demand validation

3. **Product/Solution**
   - Uniqueness of the solution and competitive advantages
   - Product-market fit or evidence of traction
   - Technology or innovation differentiation
   - Scalability of the product

4. **Business Model/Economics**
   - Revenue model and path to profitability
   - Unit economics and margins
   - Capital efficiency and burn rate
   - Competitive moat and defensibility

## Application:

VCs use this framework to:
- Create a systematic evaluation process across all deals
- Identify strengths and weaknesses in investment opportunities
- Compare different opportunities on standardized criteria
- Reduce bias in decision-making
- Communicate investment rationale to partners

The framework recognizes that exceptional strength in one area might compensate for weakness in another, but typically successful investments need to score well across multiple lenses. Different VC firms may weight these lenses differently based on their investment philosophy and stage focus.
